\begin{alertblock}{Introduction}

The ultimate objective of basektball is to score more points than the opponent. Consequentially, a fundamental challenge in basketball analytics is determining who the best scorers are. However, not all shot attempts are created equal. A wide-open dunk in transition, an open catch-and-shoot corner three-pointer, and a contested midrange stepback all have different expected conversion rates. Treating them identically conflates \textbf{shot quality} with \textbf{shooting skill}.

\vspace{2ex}

%\begin{center}
%\begin{tabular}{cc}
%\includegraphics[width=0.42\linewidth]{img_dunk.jpg} &
%\includegraphics[width=0.42\linewidth]{img_contested3.jpg} \\
%{\small Wide-open dunk} & {\small Contested deep three}
%\end{tabular}
%\end{center}

%\vspace{0.5ex}

Despite their importance for player evaluation, common public metrics such as FG\%, eFG\%, and even TS\% are heavily confounded by shot selection and defensive assignment. These are simple summaries of shooting outcomes that are much more context-dependent than implied by a single percentage.

\vspace{2ex}

We introduce \textbf{Expected Points Above Average (EPAA)}, a two-stage framework that:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Estimates \textit{shot quality} using contextual features.
\item Estimates \textit{shooting talent} via Bayesian hierarchical modeling.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{2ex}

EPAA produces \textbf{league-adjusted metrics} for shooters, individual defenders, and team defense, such that 0 is league-average. This work uses public NBA tracking data from approximately 200,000 field goal attempts during the 2024–25 regular season, accessed via the \texttt{hoopR} R package.

\end{alertblock}

% METHODOLOGY
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{block}{Methodology}

\textbf{Stage 1: Shot Quality} \\[0.5ex]
\begin{itemize}
\item Expected Field Goal Percentage (xFG) is estimated using three separate XGBoost classifiers (one per shot type: rim, mid-range, three-point), trained on context features: shot distance, shot angle, defender proximity, dribble count, shot clock, player fatigue, and play type indicators.
\item Player and defender identities are intentionally excluded in these models, so they describe: \textit{"How hard was this shot for a league-average player guarded by a league-average defender?"}
\end{itemize}

\vspace{2ex}

\textbf{Stage 2: Shooting Talent} \\[0.5ex]
\begin{itemize}
\item Residual variation of shot difficulty is modeled using a Bayesian hierarchical model in Stan with random effects for shooters, individual defenders, and defensive teams.
\item Partial pooling shrinks low-volume players toward the league mean while preserving separation for high-volume stars, producing skill parameters with uncertainty quantification rather than simple point estimates.
\end{itemize}




\end{block}

\begin{block}{Stage 2: EPAA Bayesian Hierarchical Model}

For each shot attempt $n = 1, \dots, N$, let:
\[
\begin{aligned}
  y_n &\in \{0,1\} & &\text{shot outcome (make/miss)} \\
  t_n &\in \{1,2,3\} & &\text{shot type (rim, mid-range, three-point)}
\end{aligned}
\]

\vspace{1ex}

\textbf{Likelihood and Linear Predictor}
\[
\begin{aligned}
  y_n \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}\!\bigl(\mathrm{logit}^{-1}(\eta_n)\bigr) \\
  \eta_n = \underbrace{\alpha_{t_n}
    + \beta_{t_n} \cdot \mathrm{xfg\_logit}_n
    }_{\text{Stage\,1 prediction}}
    + \underbrace{a^{\mathrm{p}}_{j[n],\,t_n}
    }_{\text{shooter skill}}
    + \underbrace{a^{\mathrm{f}}_{d[n],\,t_n}
    }_{\substack{\text{individual} \\ \text{defender}}}
    + \underbrace{a^{\mathrm{m}}_{k[n],\,t_n}
    }_{\substack{\text{team} \\ \text{defense}}}
\end{aligned}
\]

The intercept $\alpha_t$ and slope $\beta_t$ recalibrate the Stage~1 output per shot type. Three sets of \textbf{random effects} decompose residual skill.

\vspace{1ex}

\textbf{Random Effects}
\[
\begin{aligned}
  a^{\mathrm{p}}_{j,t} &= \mu^{\mathrm{vol}}_t
    \cdot x^{\mathrm{vol}}_{j,t}
    + \sigma^{\mathrm{p}}_t \cdot z^{\mathrm{p}}_{j,t},
    &z^{\mathrm{p}}_{j,t}
    &\sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, 1) \text{ for all players } j = 1,\dots,J \\[4pt]
  a^{\mathrm{f}}_{d,t}
    &= \sigma^{\mathrm{f}}_t
    \cdot z^{\mathrm{f}}_{d,t},
    &z^{\mathrm{f}}_{d,t}
    &\sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, 1) \text{ for all defenders } d = 1,\dots,D  \\[4pt]
  a^{\mathrm{m}}_{k,t}
    &= \sigma^{\mathrm{m}}_t
    \cdot z^{\mathrm{m}}_{k,t},
    &z^{\mathrm{m}}_{k,t}
    &\sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, 1) \text{ for all teams } k = 1,\dots,K 
\end{aligned}
\]

where $x^{\mathrm{vol}}_{j,t}$ is standardized $\log(1 + \text{FGA per 36\,min})$ for player $j$ in shot type $t$.

\vspace{1ex}

\textbf{Priors}
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \alpha_t &\sim \mathcal{N}(0,\; 0.25^2)
    & \beta_t  &\sim \mathcal{N}^{+}(1,\; 0.25^2) \\[4pt]
  \mu^{\mathrm{vol}}_t &\sim \mathcal{N}(0,\; 0.3^2) \\[4pt]
  \sigma^{\mathrm{p}}_t
    &\sim \mathcal{N}^{+}\!\bigl(0,\; s^{\mathrm{p}}_t\bigr),
    &s^{\mathrm{p}}
    &= (0.30,\; 0.30,\; 0.25) \\
  \sigma^{\mathrm{f}}_t
    % &\sim \mathcal{N}^{+}(0,\; 0.20) \;\; \forall\, t \\
    &\sim \mathcal{N}^{+}\!\bigl(0,\; s^{\mathrm{p}}_t\bigr),
    &s^{\mathrm{p}}
    &= (0.20,\; 0.20,\; 0.20) \\
  \sigma^{\mathrm{m}}_t
    &\sim \mathcal{N}^{+}\!\bigl(0,\; s^{\mathrm{m}}_t\bigr),
    &s^{\mathrm{m}}
    &= (0.12,\; 0.10,\; 0.06)
\end{aligned}
\]

% {\small Family-specific prior scales encode basketball domain knowledge: $s^{\mathrm{player}}$ and $s^{\mathrm{team}}$ vary by shot family because defensive scheme effects are largest at the rim and smallest on threes, while individual defender impact ($\sigma^{\mathrm{def}}_t$) uses a common scale across all families. The half-normal $\mathcal{N}^{+}$ constrains variance parameters to be positive. The slope prior $\beta_t \sim \mathcal{N}^{+}(1, 0.25^2)$ is centered at the identity transform, encoding the belief that a better Stage~1 prediction should correspond to a higher make probability.}

\end{block}

\textbf{Key findings:}
\begin{itemize}
\item EPAA re-ranks players substantially compared to raw eFG\%, rewarding efficient shooting on difficult looks and penalizing inflated stats from easy opportunities
\item Defender rankings reveal individual rim protection skill beyond team scheme effects
\item Volume--skill relationship is family-specific: jump shot volume correlates positively with skill (selection effect), while rim volume reflects role assignment rather than finishing talent
\end{itemize}

\end{block}

\begin{block}{Conclusion \& Future Work}

EPAA demonstrates that a two-stage framework---machine learning for context, Bayesian hierarchical modeling for talent---can \textbf{separate shot difficulty from shooting skill} with principled uncertainty quantification. The crossed random effects structure (shooter $\times$ defender $\times$ team) is a novel contribution to basketball analytics.

\vspace{0.5ex}

\textbf{Limitations \& future directions:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Posterior predictive intervals are slightly overconfident at high volumes; a game-level random intercept or beta-binomial likelihood is the principled fix
\item Split-half validation (first half $\to$ second half prediction) is underway as the headline stability metric
\item \textbf{True EPAA} extension: incorporating foul-drawing value via a two-tier architecture (foul probability + ``phantom shot'' modeling)
\end{itemize}

\end{block}
